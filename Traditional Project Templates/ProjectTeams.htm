Systems Analysis

Project Teams

 

The development of  Information Systems involves working with others including owners, management, vendors, analysts, programmers, users and sometimes customers.  A major objective of this course is experiencing the team approach in which everyone contributes and “pulls their weight”. You need to develop skills in the areas of: team work, conflict management, time management and continuous improvement. In fact employers expect these skills.

A person, who has the skills to work with a variety of people, will be successful in the business world. These skills will also be beneficial in your personal life when you are working with others.  Working in a team will not always be easy. There will be disagreements. Listen to other ideas and opinions, even though you may not agree with them.  The more suggestions and ideas that are brought forth, the more likely you will come up with the best solution to the problem. 

"No one of us is as good as all of us!”

The class will divide themselves in to teams of 4.  Prepare a team charter for your team. Keep in mind that in the "real world" you do not always have a choice in the people or teams you work with. Once the teams are formed, you will be with the team until the end of the semester. Regular team meetings are essential to the success of the assignments.  Where possible, class time will be provided as work periods, however, other meeting times will be necessary.

 

Choose your teammates wisely. And, above all, do your best and have fun!

Team Charter

 

The team charter should specify a minimum of four or five ways the group will ensure that it operates as efficiently as possible.  This contract should also specify how conflict would be handled. 

 

  1. Each person’s role should be specified.  There is a sample team charter attached to this document.
     

    • Discussion leader/Devil’ advocate - initiates discussion, challenges opinions, guards against groupthink.  Groupthink is when team members all just want to get along and not cause any waves.  A member may withhold information to avoid conflict, but they may have a point that is valid and worth the team discussing.  Or alternatively, a classic sign of groupthink is when a different perspective is brought up and quickly shot down without discussion.  Groups need to be sure they are exploring all alternatives.  Remember it is OK to disagree, as long as you are not disagreeable.  Brainstorming techniques may help your group overcome this.

    •  Quality Control - checks work for completeness and accuracy, collects work and collates, proofreads work, keeps all files together and hands in the assignment.

    •  Administrator/Facilitator  - portions out work, assigns due dates, follows up to ensure work is being done, notifies rest of group of someone not pulling weight, gives out names and contact information for group, ensure conflict management process is followed, ensures that all members attend and participate at all meetings, guards against hidden or overt conflict, stays positive and keeps the group meeting on track including time management.
       

  2. To keep your team on track it is a good idea to document your progress on the project using the following tools:
     

    •  Agendas -  For each group meeting the topics for discussion, time allotted and persons responsible for preparing work.  This allows the team to run effective meetings and use time most effectively without getting off on tangents.

    •  Minutes -  For each meeting there should be minutes of what was discussed and decided.  That way all group members have documentation on what their responsibilities are and what decisions were made. Include in your minutes your plan for completing the assignment including each task, the estimated time required and persons responsible for meeting the deadline.
       

  3. There may be instances where conflict develops within a team.  If so, part of the learning experience is for team members to develop skills in identifying and managing conflict before it jeopardizes the project.  Teams will be allowed to make a recommendation to the instructor that a team member either be “fired” from the group (i.e. gets a zero for the report or assignment), or that the team member should receive a lower mark for the project.  If so, teams must be able to prove to the instructor that they have tried to resolve the conflict first (coaching) and, if that fails, have used progressive discipline before coming to the instructor.  Some activities which teams should consider would include:
     

    • As a first step, a coaching and counselling session where the team attempts to identify the behavioural gaps to the team member and outlines expectations for improvement.  The tone of the meeting should be non-judgmental, non-emotional.  The focus is on pointing out problem behaviour and problem solving for a specific resolution.

    • If this doesn’t work, a verbal warning to the team member that his/her continued participation is at risk.  Again, the team should specifically outline the facts and deficient behaviour and recommend changes that should occur.  A written warning should also be used at this point.

    • As a final step, a written memo to the instructor that outlines the steps that have been taken, dates they occurred on, and which contains copies of any written warnings that were given to the team member.  The memo should also contain the specific remedy requested, i.e. termination from the group or a reallocation of the marks.  The instructor may elect to meet with all or some of the group before rendering a decision.

Have each team member sign the charter. Don't hesitate to change the charter as the year progresses. I will ask to see your charter if your team has conflicts which cannot be resolved. The charter, meeting agendas and meeting minutes will be my guide to resolving the issues. The charter must be signed by all team members.

Resources

There are many resources in the Learning Resource Center and on the internet that can help you learn about group work.  One is the The Team Handbook[1] which outlines 10 common problems and solution strategies for teams. 

Common Problem

Solution Strategy

1.Floundering-  unsure of where to start or moving through project phases. 

  • Go back to project instructions do you have a clear direction.
  • Figure out what is holding up the team and resolve the issue
  • Get thinking.
  • Don’t leave it until the last minute start on a project as soon as it is assigned.** my suggestion!

2.Overbearing participants- if some members have a disproportionate amount of influence in a team.  Presents “sacred cows”, areas that are untouchable.  Regularly discounts any proposed activity by declaring that it won’t work.
  • Team reinforce there are no sacred cows
  • Speak to the overbearing participant privately and ask for cooperation and patience.
  • Deal in facts only.  “In God we trust.  All others must have data!”

3. Dominating participants- members that talk too much and discourage other members from speaking up.  Everyone should have a voice on the team or there will be problems. “Extroverts.”
  • Structure process that all members have equal participation.  When discussing ideas go around the table.
  • Practice gate keeping or use a timer to limit people time.

4. Reluctant participants- quiet crowd may say they will speak up when they have something to say.  “Introverts.”
  • Similar to above, structure process that all members have equal participation.  Facilitator- Encourage all members to participate.
  • Send out agendas ahead of time so they have time to think about and prepare contributions for discussion items.

5. Unquestioned acceptance of opinions as facts- people speak with such confidence they must know.  But not always…
  • Ask for evidence in the form of data to support the position.
  • Don’t take someone at their word without evidence.  Just because they sound confident does not always mean they are right.

6. Rush to accomplishment- a member that pushes forward to do something to accomplish task fast.  Some people do not like to analyze, but act.  Quality takes patience.
  • Provide constructive feedback to “rusher”, that is non-judgmental.  E.g. Would you help me understand how you reached that conclusion so quickly?”
  • Make sure the discussion leader is not exerting pressure for speed, lead by example.

7. Attribution- we attribute motives to people with whom we disagree or don’t understand.  This can create resentment and can mislead the team.
  • Stick to data when making decisions
  • Ask questions, when you feel this might be happening.  E.g. “Help me understand how you came to that conclusion when I say …”

8. Discounts and “plops”-Discounts are when statements are made that ridicule our values either consciously or unconsciously.  We then feel discounted and resentful.  Plops are statements someone makes and then are ignored by the team.
  • Support the discounted person.
  • Talk privately with anyone who frequently discounts or puts down or ignores previous speakers’ statements.
  • Team should discuss all input, not let anyone’s fall by the wayside.

9. Wanderlust: digression and tangents- our natural tendency to stray from the subject.
  • Facilitator needs to guard against this and get the meeting back on track.
  • Agenda can keep your group focused.
  • Direct conversation back on track.

10. Feuding team members- members that do not get along and like to fight.
  • Try to contain the feud during the meeting.
  • Have the two meet privately to sort out their differences.
  • Help the feuding members develop a contract about their behavior or ground rules for managing their differences without disrupting the team.
